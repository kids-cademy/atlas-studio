The American goldfinch is a small songbird, but one of the most widespread in North America.

These colorful birds change their plumage in the spring and autumn.

Males are shiny yellow in the summer and of olive color during the winter, while females are dull yellow-brown shade which brightens slightly only during the summer.

The autumn plumage is almost identical in both sexes, but males have yellow patches on shoulder.

The immature birds have a dull brown back, and the underside is pale yellow.

The American goldfinch is a small birds, 11 to 14 centimeters long, with a wingspan of 19 to 22 centimeters. It weighs between 11 and 20 grams.

The beak is small, conical, and pink for most of the year, but turns bright orange in the spring, in both sexes.

The shape and size of the beak aid in the extraction of seeds from the seed heads of thistles, sunflowers, and other plants.

The American goldfinch feeds during the day.

It is mainly granivorous, but occasionally hunt insects, with which its chickens are fed, to provide them protein in growth period.

Its diet consists of the seeds from a wide variety of annual plants, often those of weeds, grasses, and trees.

It eats seeds from thistle heads, teasel, dandelion, ragweed, mullein, cosmos, goatsbeard, sunflower, and alder.

However, it also consumes tree buds, maple sap, and berries.

It will eat at bird feeders provided by humans, particularly in the winter months, preferring Niger seed.

Also the hawks and cats, which pose a threat to both young and adults.

During the breeding season, American goldfinches live in loose colonies.

This species is generally monogamous, and produces one brood each year.

In the period the nest is being constructed, both male and female will act aggressively toward other intruders, driving them away.

The American goldfinch flies in a distinctive, following an undulating pattern, in a path resembling a wave.

This consists of a series of wing beats to lift the bird, then folding the wings while gliding in an arc, before repeating this sequence.

Birds often vocalize during the ascending phase and then move silently during the gliding phase.
